Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • The number of new covid cases seems to ebb and flow without a strong general directional trend (i.e. consistently increasing or consistently decreasing).
  • New covid cases seem to come in "waves" in which there are periods of time of large amounts of new covid cases.
  • The first major wave is around the start of 2021 (December 2020 - January 2021). The next major wave is around August to September of 2021. The third wave is the most recent Omnicron outbreak happening at the start of 2022.
  • It seems that the recent Omnicron outbreak will generate the largest wave of new Covid cases.
  • The number of new cases in June 2021 was parituclarly low.

Design Critique:

Ignoring the intriguing spiral design for the time dimension, many of the design choices seem to be quite effective in making the visualization readable. Firslty, I appreciated a lot of the labeling and minor design choices. Each year is labeled near the January axis with an arrow to make clear both the direction of time in this chart and where to start reading (i.e., the center). The 7-day average label is a nice detail for us to know, although maybe it could be included in the title as well. I also believe that the legend is trying to communicate that a linear scale is being used, since the 0 becomes 150k via straight lines, although this is not super clear (ideally this should be made explicit). Using width to represent the measure of interest is a good idea, given that the time dimension must use up the whole plane's dimensionality. Also, the solid border adds visibility and emphasizes the width of the spiraling curve. Lastly, the specific measure of choice, 7-day rolling averages, is an intelligent choice that eliminates weekly periodicity and random noise, so that we are mainly exposed to trends over large periods.

Now, the choice of this spiral design is a little bit confusing and suspicious to me. It does have some symbolic significance by placing 2020 in the center of the spiral, sort of communicating that this was the "origin" of the pandemic. Also, it still doesn't lose the directionality of time given the effective labeling, and it also allows for us to see if there is any intrayear seasonality, although I do not believe there seems to be any. (The number of years is likely to small for there to be any observable intrayear seasonality, anyway.) Overall, I think the spiral choice lacks sufficient motivation for it to be implemented: I think it would be better for time to a simple 1D linear dimension like in a typical line chart. The way the visualization is now, the reader is required to move their eyes in circles and is more likely to jump around different time periods with their eyes. In addition, one should note that the length of the curve corresponding to a fixed time period (e.g. 3 months) is actually increasing as time goes on. That is, time gets more and more stretched as time increases. Furthermore, this rate of stretching is nonconstant since sometimes the curvature is that of a circle centered at the origin and sometimes the distance from the origin is increasing. These inconsistencies do not make sense, in my opinion, and they add unnecessary complexity to the chart. I do not see what would be lost by switching to a simple linear line chart.

Visualization Sketches

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • As discussed in my critique, I believed that switching to a line plot would reduce unnecessary complexity, so that is what I did.
  • I think this plot was a successful improvement over the original visualization by simply being simpler and easier to read, without losing any important communicating features. The three "waves" of new covid cases are more clearly represented, as are some additional mini-waves.
  • One possible advantage of the spiral plot is that it covers more distance for a comparable area. That is, the black line (which represents the time axis) in the original plot is much longer, so I want to experiment with possible ways to lengthen this axis without just making a super wide line plot that wouldn't look good in a browser.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • I wanted to see if I could stretch out the time dimension more and if this would allow the viewer to observe more detail. I also wanted to see if I could make the original ending date a more clear "end" while still incporating data from after that date in the 2022 graph.
  • I also thought it could expose any intrayear seasonality that I may have missed from the original visualization. There wasn't any to be seen, however.
  • I think I was successful in separating data by the date 2022-01-06, but there is also a somewhat arbitrary time split at the start of 2021. In addition, the ease of reading is worse than expected; I do not like how the different time periods seem so discretely separated even with the small amount of overlap. Comparing height of the lines between the graphs is also slightly harder than before, and the eye isn not drawn to the peak in January 2022 as much anymore (since it is represnted twice, which is akward).
  • In general, I think splitting the time periods introduces more downsides than upsides. In the next sketch, I want to explore different scaling methods than linear scaling.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • I was wondering if plotting in log-scale could reveal some patterns or trends not seen before in the linear scale.
  • The log-scale sketch seems to equally emphasis peaks and troughs, so that the three discrete waves from before are no longer as emphasized. While lows of daily new covid cases could be interesting to look at, I believe the waves/peaks are a bit more important to communicate, since this is the thing that is trying to be prevented.
  • Also, the log-scale makes the highest peak in January 2022 seem much less dramatic. I am not sure, but I feel that it could mislead viewers by suggesting that the new wave of Omicron cases is not that much more signficiant than previous waves.
  • Overall, I think that the first sketch is still better, and the only concern I have is that about making the the new case data from after the publication of the original article visually different from the data that is shared with that visualization.

Final Visualization Design

Final Writeup

In my final design, I essentially just converted the original visualization into a simpler line plot where time is linear and one-dimensional. I believe that this simpler and more conventioanl line chart allows for the viewer to easier observe the severity of the Omicron variant's spike in new cases in January 2022 and compare it to previous spikes in new case numbers. I also included some annotations and a button that allows for viewers to either view the same exact data that was used in that original visualizaiton or gain insight into what realized after the original visualizaiton was released. This could be useful for direct comparison with the original figure vs. trying to gain more insight about the early 2022 Omicron spike. I also made the line become a different color after this publication date, so that it is clear on the longer-horizon chart where the new data comes in. I used the same measure in the original visualizaiton (a 7-day rolling average) since I believe that this already sufficiently reduces weekly seasonality and random noise to provide the most insight. Laslty, I decided to make a minor addition of an annotation that shows the origin of the Omicron variant, just so that the major January 2022 spike can be appropriately associated with the Omicron variant. That is, I am trying to tell the story that this new large spike, which is larger than any other spike seen before, is caused by Omicron.

In my original critique, my main issue was with the spiral design choice and how that does not appropriately represent the time dimension. I believe that my more simple visualization solves these problems by reducing complexity and allowing for easier viewing while also making the passage of time more uniform and intuitive. One thing that I did lose by doing so was the lack of length/space on a linear line chart. I had the idea of splitting the line chart up into a series of vertically stacked charts, but this did not work out as well as I hoped for. I also believe that this isn't a big problem since I do not think there are a ton of super important lower time scale observations, nor do I think such observations would be super relevant.
